About This Curriculum

A short explanation of how these seven themes are put together, and why.

Structure

Five mediums, one theme

Each of the seven themes pairs the same five things: a lecture, a film, a book, an album, and a work of art. A lecture reasons through an idea. A film dramatizes it. A book spends hundreds of pages inside one person's head. An album makes you feel it. A painting freezes it into one image. Together, they cover a theme from five different angles instead of one.

The lectures

Why university courses

The lecture in each theme comes from an actual recorded course — Michael Sandel's Justice at Harvard, Shelly Kagan's Death at Yale, Tamar Gendler's Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature at Yale, Robert Sapolsky's Human Behavioral Biology at Stanford. These universities recorded the full courses and released them free, taught by people who've spent their careers on the subject.

A tradition

What the Great Conversation is

Where a work is tagged "Classic," it's there because it's part of what's sometimes called the Great Conversation: the idea that the major works of a tradition are in dialogue with each other across centuries, each one responding to what came before. Reading Plato doesn't just teach you Plato, it also gives you the reference point that later writers assumed their readers already had. Knowing the classics means recognizing that conversation when you're in it.

An honest gap

The classics aren't diverse, and Further Exploration is where we balance that

The "Classic"-tagged works are overwhelmingly white, overwhelmingly male, and overwhelmingly Western. That's the traditional canon, and we kept it that way on purpose, for the reasons above. But it's a real gap, and we're not pretending it isn't.

Each theme's main selections stay small on purpose: otherwise this would turn into a much longer list, not something you can get through in an afternoon. The three Further Exploration picks at the bottom of each theme are where we make room for what didn't fit: different regions, genders, and traditions than the classics represent. Hannah Arendt, Robin Wall Kimmerer, Arundhati Roy, Frantz Fanon, Wangari Maathai, Nina Simone, Fela Kuti, and others who don't usually make it onto the standard syllabus. If you have the time and the curiosity, that's where to look.

The badge

What "syllabus" means

A handful of works in each theme, the ones marked "Classic," also carry a small tag:

It means we checked: this is a work genuinely assigned in university courses. It's there so that even if you never read Middlemarch, you know it's one of the books that comes up if you're in a room with people who did. Each classic book also links to a quick summary and a discussion of it, for anyone who wants to know it without reading the whole thing.

Sourcing

Where the links go

Every link under a work points to a source with a named author and an editorial process behind it, roughly in this order:

PrimaryThe museum that actually owns and studies the artwork — the Met, LACMA, the National Gallery. Their curators work directly with the physical piece, in a way no secondhand account can match.
ScholarlyPublished academic writing — a university press book, a named historian's essay — when it exists and is accessible.
EditedJournalism and criticism with an editorial process behind it: BBC's In Our Time, NPR, Rolling Stone, Britannica.
