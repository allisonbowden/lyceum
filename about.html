About This Curriculum

Structure

Five mediums, one theme

Each of the seven themes pairs the same five things: a lecture, a film, a book, a piece of music, and a work of art. A lecture reasons through an idea. A film dramatizes it. A book spends hundreds of pages inside one person's head. Music makes you feel it. A painting freezes it into one image. Together, they cover a theme from five different angles instead of one.

The lectures

Why university courses

The lecture in each theme comes from an actual recorded course: Michael Sandel's Justice at Harvard, Shelly Kagan's Death at Yale, Tamar Gendler's Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature at Yale, Robert Sapolsky's Human Behavioral Biology at Stanford, John Merriman's European Civilization at Yale. The universities recorded the full courses and released them free. Every one is taught by someone who has spent a career on the subject.

The badge

What "syllabus" means

Works marked "Classic" also carry a small tag:

Classic, 1871–72 syllabus

It means we checked: this is a work genuinely assigned in university courses. It's there so that even if you never read Middlemarch, you know it's one of the books that comes up if you're in a room with people who did. Each classic book also links to a discussion of it, for anyone who wants to know it without reading the whole thing.

Sourcing

What's behind each link

Every link under a work points to a source with a named author and an editorial process behind it, roughly in this order:

PrimaryThe museum that actually owns and studies the artwork: the Met, LACMA, the National Gallery. Their curators work directly with the physical piece, in a way no secondhand account can match.
ScholarlyPublished academic writing, like a university press book or a named historian's essay, when it exists and is accessible.
EditedJournalism and criticism with an editorial process behind it: BBC's In Our Time, NPR, Rolling Stone, Britannica.
