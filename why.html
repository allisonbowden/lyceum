Why Liberal Arts

What a liberal arts education actually is, why it still matters, and why this curriculum is built the way it is.

The idea

What "liberal arts" actually means

The term doesn't mean "the humanities." A liberal arts education spans philosophy, history, literature, science, math, and the arts together, on the idea that someone trained to reason well in one domain can carry that reasoning into any domain. The name comes from the Latin artes liberales: the skills considered necessary for a free person to think and participate in public life, as distinct from a single trade taught for its own sake.

The case for it

Why it still matters

Most degrees train you for a specific job. A liberal arts education trains something narrower and more durable: the ability to read an argument closely, weigh competing values against each other, and change your mind when the evidence calls for it. Those skills don't expire when a job market shifts, which is part of why liberal arts colleges have stayed largely the same for centuries while specific vocational programs come and go.

A tradition

What the Great Conversation is

Where a work is tagged "Classic," it's there because it's part of what's sometimes called the Great Conversation: the idea that the major works of a tradition are in dialogue with each other across centuries, each one responding to what came before. Reading Plato doesn't just teach you Plato, it also gives you the reference point that later writers assumed their readers already had. Knowing the classics means recognizing that conversation when you're in it.

The method

Why five mediums, not one

Studying an idea inside a single discipline shows you one version of it. This curriculum pairs a lecture, a film, a book, an album, and a work of art around the same theme because each medium argues differently: a lecture reasons through an idea, a film dramatizes it, a book spends hundreds of pages inside one person's head, an album makes you feel it, a painting freezes it into a single image you can return to. Seeing the same idea argued five different ways is closer to how it actually lives in a culture than any one version alone.

An honest gap

The classics aren't diverse, and Further Exploration is where we balance that

The "Classic"-tagged works are overwhelmingly white, overwhelmingly male, and overwhelmingly Western. That's the traditional canon, and we kept it that way on purpose, for the reasons above. But it's a real gap, and we're not pretending it isn't.

Each theme's main selections stay small on purpose: otherwise this would turn into a much longer list, not something you can get through in an afternoon. The three Further Exploration picks at the bottom of each theme are where we make room for what didn't fit: different regions, genders, and traditions than the classics represent. Hannah Arendt, Robin Wall Kimmerer, Arundhati Roy, Frantz Fanon, Wangari Maathai, Nina Simone, Fela Kuti, and others who don't usually make it onto the standard syllabus. If you have the time and the curiosity, that's where to look.

A personal note

I rushed past this the first time

In high school I moved through AP courses as fast as I could, mostly to bank college credit and get to my business classes sooner. The plan was to graduate faster, start working faster, become an adult faster. It worked. It also meant I never really took a philosophy class, or gave myself the kind of unhurried time a liberal arts education is actually built around.

The one exception was stumbling onto Michael Sandel's Justice lectures from Harvard, free online. I'd never engaged with philosophy in any real way before that, and it caught me off guard how much I liked it.

This site is what came out of that: putting the pieces of a liberal arts education back together myself, and actually sitting down with them.
